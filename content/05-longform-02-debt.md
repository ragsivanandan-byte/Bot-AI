### SECTION 1 — Good Debt vs Bad Debt (0:30–2:15)
**[VISUAL]** Carte de crédit vs immeuble locatif, comparaison visuelle
**VOICE:**
> First we have to fix a definition. When most people hear "debt," they think of the bad kind — credit cards at twenty percent, a car loan on a car that's losing value, financing a lifestyle you can't afford. That debt takes money *out* of your pocket every month and gives you nothing back. That kind of debt absolutely can sink you.
>
> **[B-ROLL]** Immeuble moderne, clés, contrat de prêt signé
> But there's another kind. Imagine you borrow money to buy something that puts *more* money in your pocket than the loan costs you. You take a loan at five percent to buy a property that earns you eight percent in rent. The debt is now paying for itself — and handing you the difference. That's "good debt." The rich don't avoid debt. They obsess over the *difference* between the two. Bad debt buys liabilities. Good debt buys assets that outpace the interest.
>
> **[VISUAL]** Balance dorée : "cost of debt" vs "return on asset"
> The single question they ask before borrowing is simple: *will this money earn more than it costs?* If yes, borrowing isn't risky — it's leverage. And leverage is how small money becomes big money.

### SECTION 2 — Leverage: The Multiplier (2:15–4:00)
**[VISUAL]** Petit levier soulevant un gros poids, concept, or/noir
**VOICE:**
> Let's make leverage concrete, because this is where the magic is. Say you have a hundred thousand dollars. You buy an asset, it goes up ten percent — you made ten thousand. Nice.
>
> **[B-ROLL]** Graphique : un capital vs cinq capitaux empilés
> Now say you use that same hundred thousand as a down payment, and borrow four hundred thousand more — so you control five hundred thousand worth of assets. It goes up the same ten percent. That's fifty thousand dollars of gain… on your hundred thousand. Minus the interest, you've turned a ten percent return into something far bigger. *That's* leverage. You used the bank's money to multiply your own result.
>
> **[VISUAL]** Immeuble, puis plusieurs immeubles, expansion
> This is exactly how real estate fortunes are built. Nobody buys a hundred buildings with cash. They buy one, borrow against it, use that to buy the next, and the portfolio compounds. The catch — and it's a real one — leverage multiplies losses too. If the asset *drops* ten percent, your loss is just as amplified. That's why the rich pair leverage with *cash-flowing* assets and big safety margins. Used carelessly, leverage is how people go broke. Used carefully, it's how they get rich.

### SECTION 3 — Buy, Borrow, Die (4:00–6:00)
**[VISUAL]** Coffre, actions, certificats, ambiance feutrée
**VOICE:**
> Now the strategy that sounds almost too good to be true: "buy, borrow, die." Here's how it works, step by step.
>
> **[ON-SCREEN]** "1. BUY"
> Step one — *buy*. The wealthy own appreciating assets: stocks, real estate, businesses. These grow over time, but here's the key — you're not taxed on growth until you *sell*.
>
> **[ON-SCREEN]** "2. BORROW"
> Step two — *borrow*. Instead of selling and paying tax, they take a loan using those assets as collateral. A loan isn't income, so it isn't taxed. They get cash to spend, while the assets keep growing untouched in the background. The interest on the loan is often *lower* than the tax they'd have paid by selling. So they get their money and skip the tax bill.
>
> **[ON-SCREEN]** "3. DIE"
> **[B-ROLL]** Domaine transmis, héritage, document notarial
> Step three — and this is the brutal part — *die*. In many systems, when assets pass to heirs, the taxable "clock" can reset, so a huge chunk of gains may never get taxed at all. The wealth transfers, the loans get settled from the estate, and the cycle continues with the next generation.
>
> **[VISUAL]** Cycle doré en boucle
> Buy assets. Borrow against them instead of selling. Pass them on. The poor earn, get taxed, then spend. The rich own, borrow tax-free, and let assets do the work. Same economy — completely different rules.

### SECTION 4 — What You Can Actually Use (6:00–7:15)
**[VISUAL]** Bureau élégant, réflexion, skyline
**VOICE:**
> You're probably not borrowing against a stock portfolio tomorrow — so what's the usable lesson? Three things.
>
> **[ON-SCREEN]** "1. Separate good debt from bad"
> One. Ruthlessly separate good debt from bad. Kill high-interest consumer debt — that's the trap. But stop fearing *all* debt. A mortgage on an appreciating home, or a loan for a business that earns more than it costs, is a tool, not a danger.
>
> **[ON-SCREEN]** "2. Buy assets, not just stuff"
> Two. The whole game runs on *owning assets*. You can't borrow against a designer watch or a leased car. You can borrow against things that grow. Build the asset base first — everything else is downstream of that.
>
> **[ON-SCREEN]** "3. Think in returns vs cost"
> Three. Before any loan, ask the rich person's question: will this money earn more than it costs me? If yes, it might be leverage. If no, it's just a liability with a nicer name.
