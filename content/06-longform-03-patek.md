### SECTION 1 — The Watches That Actually Appreciate (0:30–2:15)
**[VISUAL]** Montre acier sport, gros plan macro, reflets
**VOICE:**
> Let's start with the truth that fuels the whole idea: *some* watches genuinely appreciate, and spectacularly. Certain steel sports models from the top brands have tripled, even quadrupled in value over the last decade. People who bought at retail and managed to get one walked away with gains that beat the stock market.
>
> **[B-ROLL]** Vente aux enchères, main gantée présentant une montre
> And at the very top, vintage pieces sell at auction for millions. A rare reference with the right history can become one of the most valuable objects you can own per gram. So yes — the headline is real. Watches *can* be investments.
>
> **[VISUAL]** Liste de modèles "winners" surlignés en or
> But notice the pattern in every winner: they're *rare*, usually *steel*, from a *handful* of brands, and almost impossible to buy at retail. The appreciation isn't about luxury — it's about scarcity, exactly like the Rolex waitlist. The watch is only an asset because you *can't easily get one*. Keep that in mind, because it's about to explain the other ninety percent.

### SECTION 2 — The 90% Nobody Talks About (2:15–4:00)
**[VISUAL]** Montres ordinaires qui ternissent vers le gris, concept
**VOICE:**
> Here's what the slogans skip. For every watch that triples, there are far more that *lose* money the moment you leave the store. Walk into a boutique, buy a beautiful but readily-available luxury watch for fifteen thousand, try to sell it the next day — and you might get nine or ten. You just lost a third of your money, instantly. Same as driving a new car off the lot.
>
> **[B-ROLL]** Vitrine pleine de montres disponibles, abondance
> Why? Because that watch *isn't* scarce. The brand makes plenty of them, the boutique can always sell you a new one, and the second-hand market is flooded with the same reference. No scarcity, no premium. It's a depreciating luxury good — gorgeous, but a cost, not an asset.
>
> **[VISUAL]** Diagramme : 10% montent, 90% descendent
> So the honest picture is a barbell. A small slice of watches are genuinely brilliant assets. The vast majority are expensive jewelry that quietly loses value. The slogan "you're keeping it for the next generation" is true emotionally — but for most watches, you're keeping a *cost* for the next generation, not a windfall.

### SECTION 3 — Watch vs Index Fund: The Real Comparison (4:00–6:00)
**[VISUAL]** Montre vs graphique boursier, comparaison élégante
**VOICE:**
> Let's be brutal and compare a watch to a boring investment: a stock index fund.
>
> **[B-ROLL]** Graphique composé montant régulièrement
> The index fund historically returns around seven to ten percent a year, on average, hands-off, fully liquid — you can sell it in seconds. It pays no style points, but it compounds silently in the background. Put fifty thousand in, leave it twenty years, and it can quietly become two or three times that.
>
> **[VISUAL]** Montre dans un coffre, immobile
> The watch? Even a *good* one ties up your money, can't be sold instantly, may need servicing that costs thousands, and only the rare references beat the market. Most don't even keep up with inflation. As a pure *investment*, a watch is, for most people, worse than the boring index fund — less liquid, more risk, more cost, and concentrated in a few hard-to-get pieces.
>
> **[VISUAL]** Poignet avec montre, fierté discrète
> But — and here's the honest nuance — a watch gives you something the index fund never will: you can *wear* it. You enjoy it every day. It carries meaning, history, identity. An index fund is a number on a screen. So the real question isn't "is it a good investment?" It's: *are you buying an asset, or are you buying joy and telling yourself it's an asset?*

### SECTION 4 — How to Buy Smart (6:00–7:15)
**[VISUAL]** Bureau élégant, montre + carnet, réflexion
**VOICE:**
> If you do want a watch *and* want to protect your money, here's the grown-up approach. Three rules.
>
> **[ON-SCREEN]** "1. Buy it to enjoy, not to flip"
> One. Buy it because you love it and can afford it — full stop. If it holds value, that's a bonus, not the plan. People who buy purely to flip usually overpay and get burned.
>
> **[ON-SCREEN]** "2. If you want resale value, buy scarcity"
> Two. If resale value matters to you, follow the scarcity rule: the rare, steel, hard-to-get references from the top brands hold value best. The easy-to-buy pieces almost never do.
>
> **[ON-SCREEN]** "3. Never confuse a purchase with a portfolio"
> Three. Never let a watch *replace* real investing. Build your index funds and assets first. Then, if there's room, buy the watch with money you've already decided to spend on enjoyment.
